\## Post 1 — LLM Personalization Breaks Single-Prompt Visibility Tracking (1 week ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/deveshkhanal_on-the-left-is-an-actual-prompt-and-answer-activity-7473516484342726656-VANT>



On the left is an actual prompt and answer a client sent where ChatGPT recommended Grow and Convert. On the right is testing her exact prompt in an incognito ChatGPT session — nowhere to be found. Why? Because LLM responses are heavily personalized to the user, not just randomness; the response is personalized to a massive amount of context ChatGPT has about her.



What this means for GEO/AEO strategy:

1\. You can't gauge AI visibility by measuring single prompts. Even tools like Profound that claim to track prompts can't reproduce the actual personalized answer a real user gets.

2\. Measure visibility by TOPIC — a collection of 5-10 prompts all asking about the same thing — and track how often you're mentioned or cited across that cluster.

3\. LLMs recommend tools based on extreme context and detail. The only way to get LLMs to do that is to publish highly detailed bottom-of-funnel content teaching the LLMs about case studies, pain points solved, who you serve, and why you're better than alternatives.

\## Post 2 — SEO Presence Study for ChatGPT's Cited Sources (1 week ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/deveshkhanal_more-evidence-that-seo-geo-it-looks-like-activity-7472301953658564608-8b4U>



More evidence that SEO leads to GEO. The majority of sources ChatGPT cites for product-related queries are ranking for standard, predictable SEO keywords — but this doesn't mean ChatGPT just pulls top Google results.



Analyzed sources for 10 different bottom-of-funnel, product-related queries. Two big takeaways:

1\. 60% of ChatGPT's sources were ranking for topically relevant keywords. 40% had no detectable SEO presence.

2\. 100% of sources ranking for a "fan out query" were also ranking for a more predictable short SEO keyword — meaning you don't need to specifically "target" fan out queries. Just target normal SEO keywords.



Yes, ChatGPT isn't Google, but a higher percentage of its cited sources DID have an SEO presence with predictable, common-sense keywords.

\## Post 3 — Business Loan Client Case Study: Keyword Rankings + AI Visibility (3 weeks ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/deveshkhanal_1-seo-takes-time-2-seo-still-works-activity-7468051453753057280-dXvW>



SEO takes time, and SEO still works. Leads from content for a client in the ridiculously competitive business loan space, a new brand. Ranking for keywords like "marketing loans" (#1), "construction line of credit" (#1), "same day merchant cash advance" (#3), "accounts receivable funding companies" (#4), "fast invoice factoring" (#4).



These are high buying-intent keywords, not top-of-funnel vanity traffic — which is why they're generating leads, not just traffic. This is translating to growth in AI visibility across 5 different LLMs for related topics.



While people on LinkedIn talk about "schema," "llms.txt," and "optimizing FAQs," the fundamentals — identifying buying keywords humans and LLMs would both search, writing high-quality content (not gen AI slop) to rank for them, and repeating — still produce results.

\## Post 4 — SEO Tools Brand-Mention Study Across 5 LLMs (1 month ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/deveshkhanal_ok-we-asked-about-seo-tools-7-different-ways-activity-7462350595555844097-piUM>



Asked about SEO tools 7 different ways across ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, AI Overview, and AI Mode (35 total responses analyzed). Key findings:



1\. SEMrush and Ahrefs are 1st and 2nd, mentioned in 80% of the 35 responses — consistent with research showing the most popular brands consistently get mentioned despite LLM variability.

2\. Google Search Console is the 3rd most mentioned "SEO tool" by LLMs, despite "SEO is dead" narratives.

3\. Surfer and Clearscope rank 5th and 6th, ahead of older incumbents like Screaming Frog and Moz.

4\. Reddit is the top cited domain supporting these answers — though a separate study found 80% of citations generally come from industry-specific sites, not general sites like Reddit or Wikipedia.

5\. Brand-owned listicle posts remain among the most cited URLs — "self-promotional listicles" aren't dead yet.
