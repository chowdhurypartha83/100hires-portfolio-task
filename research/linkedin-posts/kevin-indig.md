\## Post 1 — Off-Property Reputation Drives AI Citation Gaps (1 week ago)

https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig\_two-brands-can-run-identical-on-page-optimization-activity-7473336241837731841-Dl3f



Two brands can run identical on-page optimization and get cited by AI at completely different rates. The gap is off-property reputation. AI reuses the trust already attached to the sources it pulls from, and it favors entities it already recognizes on a topic. It doesn't form a fresh opinion about your brand on every query.



Your owned blog feeds that, but it's one of the weaker inputs. The model has no reason to take your word for your own authority. The publications, analysts, and competitors that mention you give it something it can trust without asking you.



With optimization roughly equal, the brand that wins is the one AI already trusts through other sources. Before you tune another title tag, check which sources AI cites in your space and whether any of them mention you.

\## Post 2 — Topic-Specific AI Citation Data (1 week ago)

https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig\_ai-trusts-a-different-set-of-sources-for-activity-7472249744724156417-dz3F



AI trusts a different set of sources for every topic. There's no fixed list it pulls from. The off-property authority you build has to be topic-specific, too.



I looked at AI citations across a sample of topics. For invoicing questions, competitor domains hold 33.5% of what AI cites. For starting a business, the same source type holds 7%. So building mentions everywhere wastes your budget. Lift a PR plan from a neighboring topic, and you miss the set AI actually pulls for yours.



Owned content still matters, but it's one of your weaker inputs. Map the specific domains AI already cites for your topic, then earn your way into that set.

\## Post 3 — Prompt Tracking Sample Design Framework (2 weeks ago)

https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig\_good-prompt-tracking-starts-with-sample-design-activity-7470197035565002752-T8ow



Good prompt tracking starts with sample design. For your average B2B SaaS CRM category, start with \~40 seed prompts: 12 brand, 12 category, and 16 problem prompts. The problem prompts matter because that's where purchase intent usually lives.



Run each prompt 5x per platform every week. ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews should each get their own score because aggregation hides how differently the engines behave.



The panel also needs personas — CFO, IT, and marketing buyers evaluate the same CRM category through different criteria, so averaging them together hides where a brand actually wins or loses. The metric set should include mention rate, citation rate, average position, sentiment, and the attributes attached to each mention, with confidence intervals.

\## Post 4 — "Intent Compression" from AI Overviews (3 weeks ago)

https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig\_for-20-years-what-you-searched-told-google-activity-7467298708678840320-3gvl



For 20 years, what you searched told Google and SEOs how you'd behave. Type a brand name (navigational search), and you're in and out in seconds. Search "best CRM for startups" (comparison search), and you settle into a set of comparison pages. Intent sorted everyone.



The AIO erased that tell. By dropping a block of answer text at the top of the page, it pulls every searcher into a reading session, no matter why they came. The brand-name searcher reads the AIO. The product researcher reads another. Both slow down, both stay, both behave alike on the SERP page. That flattening is the intent compression.
