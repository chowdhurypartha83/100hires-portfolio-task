\## Post 1 — Prompt Tracking Selection Methodology (7 hours ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/iamalexbirkett_how-do-you-choose-which-prompts-to-track-activity-7476227820239757314-nizO>



How do you choose which prompts to track for AI search? If they're not calibrated to reality, you'll spend a lot of time and money optimizing in an unproductive direction. Off-the-shelf prompt generators are great at creating plausible queries, but not as good at creating buyer queries.



The filter used before a prompt makes it into a tracked set:

\- Is there real demand? If nobody is searching, asking, or discussing it, tracking it is vanity.

\- Are buyers specifically asking this? People with budget and intent.

\- Can we realistically win it? Do brands like us show up today, and is there a content/citations path to visibility?

\- Do we have a representative sample of prompts to reduce variance?



This starts with buyer research and voice-of-customer research (1st and 3rd party), calibrated against market research, category entry points, and keyword research. Don't think there's a perfect system yet — the goal is to mirror the customer journey so "visibility" represents something that matters.

\## Post 2 — Third-Party Signals vs. First-Party Content (1 day ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/iamalexbirkett_anyone-can-game-what-they-say-about-themselves-activity-7475869158866452480-lu15>



Anyone can game what they say about themselves. It's much harder to fake what others say about them, especially at scale — that's the biggest "aha" in AI search.



In classic SEO, first-party content (blog, landing pages, docs) carries a lot of weight. In AI search, first-party is table stakes. The differentiator is third-party signals: G2/marketplace reviews, Reddit threads, community posts, listicles/vendor roundups, independent comparisons. LLMs synthesize consensus, and consensus is rarely found on your homepage.



A useful litmus test for marketing leaders: "Do we have a plan to monitor and engineer third-party mentions at scale?" If the answer is no, you don't have an AI search strategy yet — you have an on-site content plan.

\## Post 3 — May Client Data: AI Traffic Gains and Measurement Caveats (1 week ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/iamalexbirkett_reviewed-client-account-data-for-may-big-activity-7471882887819100160-4D7n>



Reviewed client account data for May. Across all accounts, ChatGPT referrals +66% and LLM sessions +50%. An enterprise observability brand saw AI-driven +70% sessions / +80% conversions. An enterprise GRC brand reported massive growth in referral sessions and conversions from AI.



Important caveat: ChatGPT reversed the citation compression it introduced in Q1, reintroducing direct brand URL links inline in responses at roughly 5x the previous rate — meaning some of this lift reflects ChatGPT's own product changes, not necessarily relative SEO/AEO performance.



No single metric tells the whole story, so the approach is to look at metric constellations and run experiments to separate intervention signal from macro-noise, focusing on company-level objectives over daily fluctuations.

\## Post 4 — AI Search as a "Multiplayer Reputation System" (4 weeks ago)

<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/iamalexbirkett_ai-search-turns-brand-perception-into-a-multiplayer-activity-7466084696029024257-btsC>



AI search turns brand perception into a multiplayer reputation system, not just a rankings game. With SEO, you're competing for a finite slice of SERP inventory — zero sum. With AI search, it's not just competitors that affect your perception in AI answers.



If you have a product or customer experience problem, Reddit now impacts how you're spoken about in AI answers. If you have an employee experience problem, Glassdoor now impacts your answers for "\[brand] reviews." No amount of on-page optimization solves that.



Some small, scrappy, amoral brands are scaling comparison pages with negative, sometimes inaccurate positioning about incumbents. Sometimes it's benign — e.g., AI-generated content incorrectly attributing details about who founded Omniscient. It's multiplayer game theory: not just about visibility, but defending your positioning in a complex landscape.
