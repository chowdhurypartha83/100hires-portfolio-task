\## Workflow Summary



\*\*Pre-step: Set editorial context.\*\* Keeps editorial instructions, reference files, and writing examples inside a dedicated ChatGPT project so tone/audience/standards don't need re-explaining each time.



\*\*Step 1 — Decide how much AI should handle.\*\* Categorizes topics into low-risk (stable, easy to verify — AI can do more), medium-risk (fast-changing, needs interpretation — verify sources closely), and high-risk (original research, unpublished data, legal/medical claims — AI should not lead).



\*\*Step 2 — Validate the topic before committing.\*\* Uses keyword research tools to confirm real search demand and understand the competitive landscape before writing.



\*\*Step 3 — Define the article's position before writing.\*\* A keyword describes a subject; a position gives the article something to argue. Without a clear stance, AI defaults to the safest, most generic version of a topic.



\*\*Step 4 — Build a source pack.\*\* Organizes sources into primary (official docs, original research, internal data), strong secondary (industry publications, case studies), and discovery sources (Reddit, LinkedIn, forums — used for finding questions/disagreements, not as final evidence).



\*\*Step 5 — Write a decision brief before requesting an outline.\*\* A structured brief covering goal, audience, central argument, unique angle, approved evidence, and explicitly what claims to avoid — so editorial decisions are made before AI starts writing.



\*\*Step 6 — Create and approve the outline before drafting.\*\* Reviews structure, logic order, and missing pieces before any drafting begins — fixing a weak outline takes minutes; fixing the same problem after a full draft takes much longer.



\*\*Step 7 — Write one section at a time, not the whole article at once.\*\* Longer AI-generated drafts in one pass tend to drift from the brief, repeat patterns, and lose alignment with earlier constraints.



\*\*Step 8 — Assemble and ask AI to critique the full piece.\*\* Requests a structural critique (repetition, gaps, contradictions, unsupported claims) before any rewriting — treating it as a diagnostic step, not a polish pass.



\*\*Step 9 — Make the article "mine."\*\* Reviews for generic, interchangeable passages and replaces them with specific real examples, data, or judgment calls — the core human-authorship pass.



\*\*Step 10 — Run a final claim audit.\*\* Separately verifies every statistic, comparison, or factual claim against original sources rather than trusting AI's summary of them.



\*\*Step 11 — Optimize for search only after the argument is finalized.\*\* Avoids starting with keyword optimization, which tends to distort the structure toward competitor coverage rather than reader needs.



\*\*Step 12 — Build internal links in both directions.\*\* Connects the new article to existing site content, and updates older pages to link back to it.



\*\*Step 13 — Monitor post-publication performance,\*\* including AI Overview visibility, not just traditional rankings — and feeds that data back into future topic/structure decisions.



\## Key Takeaway

The author's core thesis: the quality of AI-assisted content depends less on the model itself and more on the editorial decisions, research discipline, and judgment applied around it. The final approval, sourcing decisions, and personal examples are deliberately never delegated to AI.
